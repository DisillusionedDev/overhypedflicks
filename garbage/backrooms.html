Backrooms (2026) — A Creepypasta Stretched Beyond Its Limits

By Disillusioned Dev • Published June 2, 2026
3/10
Overall Score
A24 expands a brilliant, naturally unsettling 5-minute YouTube short into a feature-length cinematic commercial for damp, yellow-tinted office carpets.

Story & Writing 1.0/10

The fundamental issue here is scale. What works beautifully as an atmospheric, conceptual video on a monitor completely falls apart when forced into a standard three-act structure. Screenwriter Will Soodik tries to inject an architectural lore system and deep personal trauma into an entity that was scary precisely *because* it had no lore and no human baggage.

We follow an uninspiring therapist searching for a patient who slipped out of our physical dimension. What follows is nearly two hours of wandering around corridors, dealing with heavy-handed monologues that kill the mystery entirely.

Directing & Visuals 5/10

Kane Parsons is clearly an exceptionally talented visual artist, but directing a feature-length live-action movie requires a different skill set. The lighting is an accurate, sickening fluorescent yellow, which captures the original vibe well for about fifteen minutes. After an hour, it becomes an eye-straining mess.

The practical set pieces look vast, but the film relies far too heavily on repetitive visual looping. Seeing a character turn the exact same corner fifty times isn't avant-garde horror; it's simply padding the runtime.

Acting & Chemistry 4/10

Chiwetel Ejiofor handles the script with the utmost professionalism, giving his character a level of existential dread the text doesn't actually earn. Renate Reinsve is criminally underutilized, stuck playing a character whose primary narrative function is to look increasingly confused by wallpapers.

There is absolutely zero organic chemistry here because the environment is designed to alienate humans. The actors feel completely detached from one another, performing lines into a void.

Pacing & Sound 2/10

The sound design tries to coast entirely on the low, consistent hum of old lights. While minimalist dread works in a short form factor, a feature-length theatrical release needs musical momentum. Instead, the pacing crawls to an absolute halt in the middle tier of the film.

Announcing an extended, longer cut containing 15 additional minutes before the original standard print even leaves theaters feels like an absolute joke. It is already painfully bloated.

The Bottom Line

Backrooms proves that internet virality rarely translates into effective theatrical structure. It takes a phenomenal abstract concept and updates it into a mundane, bloated cinematic chore.
